I am currently an assistant professor at the Kenan-Flagler Business School at the University of North Carolina. My research interests are at the confluence of strategy, entrepreneurship, and cognition. Prior to my academic career, I worked as an IT consultant where I leveraged my background in both business and computer science to translate client needs into developer tasks.

Research
======
My research interests are at the confluence of strategy, entrepreneurship, and cognition. Specifically, I study the processes by which executives form and adapt strategy in entrepreneurial firms and nascent industries. In my research I seek to examine how executives tackle the challenge of strategy formation (descriptively) as well as which practices improve performance (normatively). This agenda has been generously supported by a SRF dissertation scholarship and later a SRF Research in Strategic Management grant. I believe that if we better understand the problem-solving capabilities and strategic decision-making processes successful entrepreneurs use to make strategic decisions and learn, then we can better advise and teach the next generation of innovative thinkers who will lead technology and our economy forward. 

I am addressing this critical area of research via two separate but related streams of work which help to unpack the interplay between how entrepreneurs think about strategy and how they learn from action. I first posited this dual-prong framework for discussing the processes entrepreneurs use to form and adapt strategy in the paper “Strategy Formation in Entrepreneurial Settings: Past Insights and Future Directions” (published in Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal in 2017, with Eisenhardt and Bingham) in which I review prior work on executive decision making and strategy formation processes and how they affect the strategy and performance of firms. I present a novel organization of prior work into either “strategizing by doing” or “strategizing by thinking.” “Doing” allows executives to learn about novel situations quickly and effectively while “thinking” allows executives to holistically design complex solutions with high internal fit. By organizing work in this way, I highlight the need to manage both novelty and complexity in the strategy formation process and the difficulty of doing both at the same time. 

For a larger overview of my work please see my [CV](https://timothyott.github.io/files/OttCV-07082026.pdf "CV") or [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=3VCchNAAAAAJ&hl=en "Google Scholar") page. 

Teaching
======
I have taught three classes at UNC across three levels of students: strategic management for the undergraduate program, strategic analysis for the full-time MBA students, and the qualitative methods seminar for our PhD students. My experiences at UNC thus far have helped me to refine my teaching philosophy around three core components: promoting student engagement in a variety of ways, teaching skills that transfer to other settings, and learning with and from my students. I am currently working with the undergraduate program to develop a new introductory entrepreneurship class and will be teaching corporate strategy to our undergraduates in the spring. 

Additionally, I have mentored students for their undergraduate honors theses and served on seven different PhD committees.
